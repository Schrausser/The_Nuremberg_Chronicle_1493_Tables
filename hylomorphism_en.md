# Hylomorphism

Initial indications of unambiguous *hylomorphic* thought processes can be found in ***Plato's*** *dialogues* (c.f. Ficinus & Grynaeus [1546](https://doi.org/10.3931/e-rara-5114) or Cooper & Hutchinson, [1997](https://doi.org/10.1017/S0009840X98410035)), particularly in relation to his presentation of (1) *change* and *identity*[^1], (2) *origin* of the *universe*[^2] and (3) the *metaphysical* division of *reality*[^3] (see Gerson, [2022](https://doi.org/10.1515/rhiz-2022-0002)).

Hylomorphism, as a theory about *material objects*, was first explained by ***Aristotle***[^4] (c. 384 – 322 BC) in *Metaphysics, Book VII* (see Aristotle, [1325](https://digi.vatlib.it/view/MSS_Borgh.236) or Lawson-Tancred, [1999](https://biblio.co.uk/9780140446197)). He  conceives every *physical entity* as a compound of ὕλη (hyle) ***matter***[^5] and  μορφή (morphē) ***form***, with the generic *form* as immanently *real* within the individual (see e.g. de Moerbeka, [14th c.](https://digi.vatlib.it/view/MSS_Borgh.8); Aristoteles & Clichtoveus, [1510](https://doi.org/10.3931/e-rara-25809) or Aristoteles & Theophrastus, [1608](https://doi.org/10.3931/e-rara-56887)). 

***Plato*** describes the true nature, the true *essence* (the *form*) as ***separate*** from the *physical* (the *matter*), ***Aristotle***, on the other hand, describes the *form* of an object as ***determining*** for its *necessary nature* (*essentialism*).

***Plato*** holds the view that the *forms* are completely ***separated*** from the *material world* which they characterize, while ***Aristotle*** argues that *form* and *matter* are generally ***inseparable*** (*separability*).

It was in the Middle Ages that *hylomorphism* became the dominant view, with scholastics of the time developing elaborate *hylomorphic* theories to explain the *world*, using the concept of ***matter*** and ***form*** to represent and explain various *theological* doctrines (c.f. Skrzypek, [2017](https://doi.org/10.1111/phib.12104), p. 360; Koslicki [2008](https://philpapers.org/rec/KOSTSO-2); Manning [2013](http://www.jstor.org/stable/43291297); Carter [2019](https://doi.org/10.1017/9781108646321.001); Simpson, [2023](https://doi.org/10.1017/9781009026475)).

[^1]: In *Phaedo* (102d-103a), it is described how a *person* remains the same *individual* despite changes, like a continuing object (*matter*) that assumes different properties (*forms*).  
[^2]: In *Timaeus*, the creator god orders or configures pre-cosmic *matter* with *forms* and numbers.  
[^3]: The division of reality in *Philebus* (23c-e) with the *unlimited* (*matter*) and the *limiting* (*form*).  
[^4]: Although Plato's (c. 428–347 BC) own view of *material objects* can to some extent be considered *hylomorphic* (cf. Gerson, [2022](https://doi.org/10.1515/rhiz-2022-0002)).  
[^5]: Also *wood*.
