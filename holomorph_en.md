# Holomorphism and Holomorph[^1]

<br>

## 1. Function Theory:

*Holomorphic functions* (Cauchy, [1825](https://archive.org/details/mmoiresurlesin00cauc/page/n4/mode/1up); Riemann, [1851](https://www.digitale-sammlungen.de/de/view/bsb10977332?page=3), [1894](http://eudml.org/doc/144444); Weierstraß, [1854](http://eudml.org/doc/147563), [1856](http://eudml.org/doc/147653)) are complex-valued functions, i.e. *mappings* from complex numbers *to* complex numbers (c.f. Ahlfors & et al., [1953](http://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctt1b9x21c);  Ullrich, [1988](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-663-06846-4_19), [2005](https://doi.org/10.1016/B978-044450871-3/50115-7); Remmert,  [1991](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4612-0939-3_1); Remmert & Schumacher, [2002a](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-56281-5_10); Bonneau & Mazzilli, [2026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bulsci.2025.103761)).

A key property of *holomorphism* is its angle preservation, a ***conformal mapping***. Thus, a rectangular lattice is transformed into its image by a *holomorphic* function; while the lattice rotates under the *mapping*, the right angles between the *mapped* line pairs are preserved (c.f. Atsuji, [1999](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0007-4497(99)00108-6); Levy et al., [2002](https://doi.org/10.1145/566570.566590); Remmert & Schumacher,  [2002b](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-56281-5_4)).

## 2. Group theory:

A subfield of *algebra* for the study of (1) group structures, for solvability of algebraic equations (Galois, [1829](https://www.numdam.org/item/AMPA_1828-1829__19__294_0/); Abel & Galois, [1889](http://resolver.sub.uni-goettingen.de/purl?PPN516765388)), and *later* through the investigation of (2) symmetries in *geometry* (Klein, [1882](https://archive.org/details/ueberriemannsthe00kleirich/page/n1/mode/1up); Lie, [1890](https://archiv.saw-leipzig.de/saw-archive/publikationen-quellen/publikationen/berichte-ueber-die-verhandlungen-der-koeniglich-saechsischen-gesellschaft-der-wissenschaften-zu-leipzig-mathematisch-physische-klasse-bd-1/berichte-ueber-die-verhandlungen-der-koeniglich-saechsischen-gesellschaft-der-wissenschaften-zu-leipzig-mathematisch-physische-klasse-bd-42/sophus-lie-ueber-die-grundlagen-der-geometrie-erste-abhandlung)).

### 2.1. Group:

A *set* $G$ of elements, together with a *operation* $*$, which assigns a third element of the same set $G$ to any two elements of the *set* $G$ (s. Specht, [1956](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-94667-7); Robinson, [1996](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4419-8594-1)).

For example, the *set* $G$ of integers $\mathbb Z$ $\\\{...,-2,-1,0,1,2,...\\\}$ with *addition* as the *operation* $∗$. For two integers $a$ and $b$, the sum $a+b$ is again an integer $c$, where the *inverse* element of $a$ is denoted as $−a$.

Thus, a *group* is an ordered pair $(G,∗)$ consisting of a *set* $G$ ($\mathbb Z$) and an inner binary *operation* $∗$ (the *addition*) on $G$.

### 2.2. Holomorph:

The *holomorph* $Hol(G)$ of *group* $G$ is that *group* which contains both (1) $G$ itself and its *automorphism group* (2) $Aut(G)$, the *mapping* between two structures of a mathematical object[^2] onto itself[^3] (cf. Burnside, [1897](https://archive.org/details/in.ernet.dli.2015.168629/mode/1up)).

[^1]: from ὅλος (holos) *whole* and μορφή (morphē) *form*.  
[^2]: *Isomorphism* from ἴσος (ísos) *equal* and μορφή (morphḗ) *form*, a *mapping* between two mathematical structures by which parts of one structure are *bijectively* (invertibly uniquely) *mapped* to equivalent parts of another structure.  
[^3]: *Automorphism* from αὐτός (autos) *self* and μορφή (morphē) *form*, an *isomorphism* of a mathematical object *onto itself*.
