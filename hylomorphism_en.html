Hylomorphism

Initial indications of unambiguous hylomorphic thought processes can be found in Plato's dialogues (c.f. Ficinus & Grynaeus 1546 or Cooper & Hutchinson, 1997), particularly in relation to his presentation of (1) change and identity1, (2) origin of the universe2 and (3) the metaphysical division of reality3 (see Gerson, 2022).

Hylomorphism, as a theory about material objects, was first explained by Aristotle4 (c. 384 – 322 BC) in Metaphysics, Book VII (see Aristotle, 1325 or Lawson-Tancred, 1999). He conceives every physical entity as a compound of ὕλη (hyle) matter5 and μορφή (morphē) form, with the generic form as immanently real within the individual (see e.g. de Moerbeka, 14th c.; Aristoteles & Clichtoveus, 1510 or Aristoteles & Theophrastus, 1608).

Plato describes the true nature, the true essence (the form) as separate from the physical (the matter), Aristotle, on the other hand, describes the form of an object as determining for its necessary nature (essentialism).

Plato holds the view that the forms are completely separated from the material world which they characterize, while Aristotle argues that form and matter are generally inseparable (separability).

It was in the Middle Ages that hylomorphism became the dominant view, with scholastics of the time developing elaborate hylomorphic theories to explain the world, using the concept of matter and form to represent and explain various theological doctrines (c.f. Skrzypek, 2017, p. 360; Koslicki 2008; Manning 2013; Carter 2019; Simpson, 2023).

1: In Phaedo (102d-103a), it is described how a person remains the same individual despite changes, like a continuing object (matter) that assumes different properties (forms).
2: In Timaeus, the creator god orders or configures pre-cosmic matter with forms and numbers.
3: The division of reality in Philebus (23c-e) with the unlimited (matter) and the limiting (form).
4: Although Plato's (c. 428–347 BC) own view of material objects can to some extent be considered hylomorphic (cf. Gerson, 2022).
5: Also wood.
